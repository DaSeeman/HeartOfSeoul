Korean Alphabet

The Korean alphabet, known as Hangul, was invented in the 15th century during the reign of King Sejong the Great. Before Hangul’s creation, Koreans used Classical Chinese characters (Hanja), which were complex and difficult for common people to learn. To promote literacy, King Sejong commissioned scholars to develop a simpler and more accessible writing system. In 1443, Hangul was created, consisting of 14 consonants and 10 vowels. This alphabet was designed to reflect the sounds of spoken Korean, enhancing communication and education among all social classes.

Hangul is a phonetic alphabet, similar to the English alphabet, but it uses a combination of 24 letters (14 consonants and 10 vowels), arranged in syllabic blocks rather than in a linear sequence. Each block represents a syllable, making the writing system visually distinct. Hangul's logical structure and phonetic consistency make it easier to learn compared to English, which has irregular spelling rules.
